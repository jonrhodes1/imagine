About Us

IMAGINE did not start as a product. It started as a conversation.

IMAGINE did not start as a product. It started as a conversation.

In seminar rooms at the University of Plymouth, across research papers, whiteboards, and the occasional "what if we tried this...", a group of psychologists kept returning to the same idea:

People do not just think in words.
They think in images.

But, most of education, performance training, and assessment treats imagination as invisible and the same for everyone.

IMAGINE was built to change that. We all think and imagine in unique ways.

Where it comes from

IMAGINE is rooted in decades of research into mental imagery, motivation, and cognition.

At the centre of this work is Professor Jackie Andrade, whose research has shaped how we understand sensory imagery and how it can be measured and applied. For example her work on the Plymouth Sensory Imagery Questionnaire helped show that imagery is not just visual, but multisensory and deeply personal.

Alongside this, Dr Julie Ji's research explores how imagery influences emotion, future thinking, and decision-making. Her work shows that imagined experiences can shape how we feel and act, even when those events have not happened.

These foundations were brought together with applied work in performance psychology, where imagery is used to understand thinking, and ways to change it.

How it was built

IMAGINE was developed by Dr Jonathan Rhodes, drawing on years of work in elite sport, military environments, healthcare, business performance, and education.

Across these settings, one pattern kept emerging:

When people could imagine what they were doing, they performed better, adapted faster, and stayed more motivated.

But there was no simple way to assess or develop that ability.

So IMAGINE was designed as a bridge between research and real-world use.

The platform was refined through collaboration with Dr Clare Walsh, Professor Jackie Andrade, Dr Julie Ji, and a wider network of researchers, students, and practitioners. Each brought a different lens: cognitive science, applied psychology, emotional processing, navigation, and teaching and learning.

The result is a shared framework.

What makes IMAGINE different

IMAGINE treats imagination as something you can observe, measure, understand, and develop.

It recognises that:

  • some people see vivid mental images
  • some think spatially without "seeing"
  • some recall lived experiences easily
  • some imagine the future more strongly than the past
  • some adapt quickly when mental scenes change
  • some build entirely new worlds

None of these are better or worse.

They are simply different ways of thinking.

Why it matters

In classrooms, workplaces, and performance environments, we often ask people to:

  • imagine outcomes
  • visualise success
  • think creatively
  • plan ahead
  • reflect on experience

But we rarely ask:

How do you imagine?

IMAGINE makes that visible.

And once it becomes visible, it becomes teachable.

A shared project

IMAGINE continues to evolve through research, teaching, and real-world use.

It is shaped by academics, students, coaches, teachers, and practitioners.

Each use adds another layer of understanding.

Because imagination is not fixed.

It is something we all build, refine, and use every day, often without noticing.

IMAGINE is where research meets practice, science meets creativity, and thinking becomes something you can see.
