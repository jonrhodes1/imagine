IMAGINE

The Framework

The Science

IMAGINE explores how people think through mental imagery. This page shows how each part of the framework connects research to real classroom practice.

What are we actually measuring?

The way we think is often the way we imagine.

Whether you're recalling the past, planning the future, solving problems, or building motivation - your brain is constantly running simulations. IMAGINE breaks those simulations down into seven key components so we can understand how people see, feel, and shape their thoughts. Each part taps into a slightly different aspect of mental imagery and cognition, grounded in psychological research.

I
Image Generation how you see thoughts

Science

Based on the Plymouth Sensory Imagery Questionnaire (PSIQ), this measures how vividly people can imagine across multiple senses - not just vision, but also sound, smell, and touch (Andrade et al., 2014).

People who generate vivid imagery tend to experience stronger emotions, clearer memories, and more engaging future thinking. Think of it as the resolution of your internal screen.

Some learners naturally generate strong multi-sensory images. Others may need support to "bring ideas to life" internally. This dimension helps identify who has a ready-made internal theatre - and who might benefit from richer stimulus and scaffolding.

Use this when teaching:

  • Descriptive writing
  • Science processes
  • Historical scenes
  • Sport technique and performance
  • Dramatic rehearsal

Try this

Ask students not only what they know, but what they can imagine seeing, hearing, or feeling about it.

  • "What does this idea look like in your mind?"
  • "What would it sound or feel like if it were real?"
M
Manipulation rotating shapes mentally

Science

Mental manipulation involves transforming images in the mind. Interestingly, individuals with aphantasia - those who report no voluntary visual imagery - can still perform well on these tasks (Zeman et al., 2010).

Visual thinking is not just about "seeing pictures" - it also involves spatial processing and mental structure. You might not see it, but you can still work with it.

A learner who struggles to "see" clearly may still be strong at:

  • Mentally rotating shapes
  • Understanding systems and structures
  • Working through spatial problems step by step

Recognising this can reframe how we support learners who seem to "struggle with imagery."

Use this when teaching:

  • Geometry and mathematics
  • Engineering and design technology
  • Movement patterns in PE
  • Maps and diagrams

Try this

Give students a shape, diagram, or process and ask them to transform it mentally before committing to paper.

  • "What would it look like turned or viewed from another angle?"
  • "Can you transform it in your mind before drawing it?"
A
Autobiographical recalling real experiences

Science

This involves deliberately reconstructing past experiences in sequence - closely linked to emotional processing, future thinking, and meaning-making (Ji et al., 2022).

How we imagine past events, especially emotionally, is connected to mood, optimism, and how we interpret our own experiences.

Learners often remember more effectively when they link ideas to lived experience. Mentally revisiting an event and rebuilding it in sequence activates a different - and often deeper - kind of understanding than simply reading back through notes.

Use this when teaching:

  • Reflective writing and portfolios
  • Fieldwork recall
  • Storytelling and narrative structure
  • Personal development sessions
  • Memory-based or experiential learning

Try this

Ask students to mentally revisit a specific moment and rebuild it in sequence. Especially useful when teaching narrative structure or reflecting on practical work.

  • "Walk back through that moment in your mind. What happened first?"
  • "What came next? How did it feel?"
G
Goal goal size and urgency

Science

Imagery plays a key role in motivation. Vividly imagined goals feel more real, more urgent, and more achievable. This is central to Functional Imagery Training (FIT), which shows that goal imagery can directly influence behaviour and performance (Rhodes & May, 2022).

When people imagine goals with sensory and emotional detail - not just as abstract statements - they are more likely to act on them.

Students differ in how they imagine the future. Some think in long-range terms; others stay close to the present step. Both are valid - but teachers can help expand the range by making short and long goals feel equally vivid and real.

Use this when teaching:

  • Revision and exam planning
  • Coursework milestones
  • Sports and performance coaching
  • Behaviour and habit change
  • Long-term project work

Try this

Ask students to imagine a future success and locate how near or far away it feels. This helps turn vague ambition into something emotionally connected and concrete.

  • "What does your goal look like?"
  • "How close does it feel right now?"
  • "What is the next step toward it?"
I
Improvise adapting mental worlds in motion

Science

Mental imagery can adapt dynamically as situations change - blending reality and imagination in real time. Research shows this kind of spontaneous imagery can amplify emotional, motivational, and moral responses to imagined events (Ji et al., 2025).

Real life rarely stays stable. The ability to adapt thinking as situations evolve is linked to creativity, emotional regulation, and decision-making under pressure.

Learners who can hold a situation in mind while it changes - without losing the thread - tend to be more flexible thinkers. This dimension helps identify who adapts naturally and who may need support when plans shift unexpectedly.

Use this when teaching:

  • Drama and performance
  • Creative and speculative writing
  • Leadership and decision-making
  • Debate and ethics
  • Problem-solving under uncertainty

Try this

Introduce a normal situation, then change one element of it. Ask students to continue imagining the scene as it shifts - supporting flexibility, creativity, and emotional regulation.

  • "What happens now that things have changed?"
  • "What do you hold onto when the scene shifts?"
  • "How do you adapt when the normal becomes strange?"
N
Navigation retracing spatial relationships

Science

This draws on two distinct spatial systems: egocentric (where things are relative to you) and allocentric (where things are relative to each other). These are actually different cognitive skills (Kozhevnikov & Hegarty, 2001).

Some people are great at rotating objects mentally; others are better at orienting themselves in space. Navigation captures how well someone can mentally position themselves and move through an environment without physically doing so.

Some learners think best through routes, positions, and orientation - rather than abstract description. Recognising spatial thinkers can open up different routes to understanding structures, systems, and complex processes.

Use this when teaching:

  • Geography and mapping
  • PE and physical movement
  • Architecture and design
  • Science models and systems
  • Sequencing and process diagrams

Try this

Give students a simple layout and ask them to imagine standing in one place, facing another, then point mentally to a third. Strengthens perspective-taking and spatial reasoning.

  • "If you were standing here, where would that be?"
  • "What changes if you turn to face the other way?"
E
Exploration building fantastical scenes

Science

This brings everything together. Exploratory imagery asks learners to construct a new imagined whole by combining elements, senses, and structure - the point where memory, perception, emotion, and creativity begin to work together.

This ability underpins problem solving, innovation, storytelling, and future planning. It's where imagination becomes possibility.

This dimension supports originality and deeper engagement with ideas. Learners who score highly here tend to move beyond recall - they generate genuinely new combinations. This is not creativity for its own sake; it's a core cognitive skill for complex problem solving and innovative thinking.

Use this when teaching:

  • Creative problem solving
  • Art and design
  • Speculative and science fiction writing
  • Future studies and innovation projects
  • Interdisciplinary and cross-subject work

Try this

Ask learners to combine familiar and unfamiliar elements into one imagined scene - then describe, draw, or narrate it.

  • "What would this look like if two impossible things were true at once?"
  • "How can you build a new world from known parts?"

Why This Matters for Teaching

Mental imagery supports both learning and thinking.

It helps pupils understand abstract ideas, remember what they have learned, imagine future goals, mentally rehearse actions, navigate problems, and build original ideas. The IMAGINE framework translates research into practical building blocks that teachers can use to better understand how learners think - and how they might be supported.

1

As a diagnostic

Helps teachers notice that students don't all think in the same way. A quiet learner who struggles to describe an image may still excel at spatial reasoning or mental orientation.

2

As a teaching tool

Each IMAGINE dimension can become a classroom strategy - from Image Generation for descriptive learning to Exploration for creative problem solving and innovation.

3

As a planning lens

Ask: does this lesson require students to imagine? What kind? Are they generating, transforming, recalling, planning, or creating? That shift makes imagination teachable rather than assumed.

Turning research into a lesson

Topic: The water cycle

Science & Application

Students need to mentally simulate movement and change over time - drawing on Image Generation, Manipulation, and Navigation. If they can imagine the journey of water, they're more likely to understand process rather than memorise labels.

Classroom prompt

  • "Imagine you are a drop of water. Where are you now? What happens next? What changes your movement?"

Topic: Exam preparation

Science & Application

This draws on Goal imagery and future simulation. Students often know what they should do, but struggle to emotionally connect with the outcome (Rhodes & May, 2022). Linking behaviour to a felt future changes that.

Classroom prompt

  • "Imagine finishing the exam knowing you gave your best. What did you do in the week before that made it possible?"
